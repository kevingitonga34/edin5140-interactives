This case study examines how Duke Energy Corporation developed and implemented a rigorous Level 1 reaction evaluation system. Beginning with basic smile sheets and evolving into a standardized electronic database used across the enterprise, Duke Energy's experience illustrates both the challenges and the payoff of treating reaction evaluation as a disciplined, purposeful practice.

The analysis below is organized around four questions that draw out the key decisions, design choices, and evaluation principles at work in the case. Each question asks you to go beyond summary and think critically about what Duke Energy's experience reveals about effective Level 1 evaluation.

Question 1 Duke Energy's move away from smile sheets was driven by an external catalyst, not an internal initiative. What does this reveal about how organizations typically come to take evaluation seriously?
+

Duke Energy had been using smile sheets for years before anyone inside the Training and Education function challenged whether they were working. The push to do something more rigorous did not come from trainers or instructional designers — it came from the Malcolm Baldrige Award criteria, an external quality framework adopted at the corporate level in the early 1990s. When that framework required the organization to produce reports on four areas of training — reaction, learning, transfer, and business results — the training function suddenly had to confront a gap it had not previously acknowledged.

This is a pattern worth recognizing because it is common. Training departments often invest in evaluation when they are required to, not when they choose to. The Baldrige criteria provided Duke Energy with both the mandate and the structure to act. What is significant is what happened next: the team discovered that their existing smile sheets were not capturing data adequate to demonstrate continuous improvement. That realization — that familiar tools were producing inadequate data — is the turning point in the case.

The lesson here is not that external pressure is bad. In this case, it was the catalyst for meaningful change. The lesson is that organizations that wait for an external mandate to evaluate seriously will always be behind organizations that build evaluation discipline into their culture from the start. Duke Energy eventually developed that discipline, but it began with a requirement, not a conviction. As you think about your own practice, consider what the equivalent of a Baldrige framework might look like in your context — and whether you would wait for it to arrive.

Question 2 Duke Energy established three primary objectives for their Level 1 evaluation system. How do these objectives reflect the principles of intentional evaluation design?
+

When Duke Energy moved beyond smile sheets, the team did not simply create a better survey. They began by articulating what the evaluation was supposed to accomplish. Their three primary objectives were: questions must measure the course, content, instructor, and relevance to the job; the form must communicate a link between quality, process improvement, and action; and action plans must be initiated to address identified weaknesses regardless of ownership or politics.

Each of these objectives reflects a deliberate choice about what evaluation is for. The first objective addresses content — what the survey asks. By naming the four areas the questions must cover, the team ensured that the instrument would produce data across all three components of Level 1 reaction: engagement, relevance, and customer satisfaction. Most traditional smile sheets collapse these into a single overall rating, which tells you very little.

The second objective addresses communication — what the survey signals to participants. Duke Energy understood that if participants do not believe their responses will be read and acted upon, the quality of the data will suffer. Building the perception of accountability into the evaluation instrument itself is a sophisticated design move, and one that is frequently overlooked.

The third objective addresses follow-through — what happens with the data. Committing in advance to act on results, without regard to political considerations, is what separates evaluation as a compliance exercise from evaluation as a tool for genuine improvement. Together, the three objectives reveal an organization that understood the difference between collecting data and using it.

Question 3 Duke Energy developed ten standardized survey questions and scaled them across the enterprise. Examine the questions themselves. What strengths do you see, and where do the questions fall short of what rigorous Level 1 evaluation requires?
+

The ten questions Duke Energy developed cover the core areas their objectives identified: overall impression, course objectives, content quality, facilities, materials, instructor knowledge, instructor delivery, time allocation, job relevance, and likelihood of recommendation. Measured on a five-point Likert scale, the instrument is clean, standardized, and practical for large-scale deployment across 23,000 employees.

Several questions stand out as genuinely useful. Question 9 — "This course relates directly to my current job responsibilities" — directly captures relevance, which is the component of Level 1 most predictive of whether learning will transfer to the job. Question 3 — "This course met the defined objectives" — invites participants to evaluate whether the training delivered on its stated promise, which is a meaningful quality indicator. Questions 4 and 5 include open-text prompts that invite qualitative elaboration, which is exactly the kind of follow-up mechanism that transforms a numeric score into actionable feedback.

However, several of the questions capture satisfaction rather than insight. Question 1 — "Overall, my impression of this course was excellent" — is essentially a global rating that does not distinguish between what drove the impression. Question 10 — "I would recommend this course to other teammates" — measures social endorsement, not learning quality. These questions are not useless, but they are the kind of items that can inflate scores without generating data you can actually act on.

What is missing from the instrument is any direct measure of engagement during the training itself, or of participant confidence and commitment to apply what they learned on the job. Both of these are components of Level 1 that matter — particularly confidence, which has been identified as a predictor of on-the-job application. Duke Energy's instrument is a meaningful improvement over a basic smile sheet, but it remains more focused on satisfaction than on the full picture of reaction that a rigorous evaluation would capture.

Question 4 By the end of 1997, Duke Energy had collected over 25,000 evaluation responses. What did the organization learn from that data, and what does the emerging correlation they identified suggest about the relationship between Level 1 and Level 3?
+

The scale of Duke Energy's data collection — growing from fewer than 200 responses in January 1996 to more than 25,000 by the end of 1997 — is significant in itself. It demonstrates what becomes possible when an organization commits to standardization. Individual evaluations are of limited value in isolation. It is only when data is collected consistently, across programs and over time, that patterns become visible and meaningful comparisons can be made.

What Duke Energy found when they analyzed that data was that reaction to training appeared to be directly linked to business management and operational aspects of the training unit. That insight led to the formation of a dedicated team to monitor both business management and education quality together — a recognition that the two could not be meaningfully separated. This is an important organizational conclusion: evaluation data, when taken seriously, does not just improve training programs. It can reshape how training is organized and managed.

Perhaps the most significant finding the case describes is Duke Energy's emerging observation of a direct correlation between Level 1 reaction and Level 3 transfer to the job. The case is careful to present this as an emerging indication rather than a proven relationship — the data at the time was not conclusive. But the hypothesis is important: if how participants react to training is predictive of whether they will apply it on the job, then Level 1 evaluation becomes far more consequential than its reputation as a satisfaction measure suggests.

This finding connects directly to a principle that runs through the Kirkpatrick framework. The levels are not isolated measurements — they are linked. A negative reaction does not guarantee that learning will fail to transfer, but it makes transfer significantly less likely. Duke Energy's data was beginning to surface the quantitative evidence for a relationship that the model has always implied. For evaluation practitioners, this is a reminder that Level 1, designed and used with care, can be a leading indicator — an early signal of what is likely to happen at higher levels.

Key Takeaways

Duke Energy's case illustrates that moving from compliance-level evaluation to meaningful evaluation is a process — one that begins with asking harder questions about what existing tools are actually measuring. The shift from smile sheets to a standardized electronic system did not happen overnight, and it was not driven by evaluation expertise alone. It required organizational commitment, clear design objectives, and the discipline to act on data rather than simply collect it.

What makes this case particularly valuable for your practice is not the technology Duke Energy built, but the thinking behind it: evaluation instruments must be designed with a clear purpose, structured to capture data you can use, and supported by processes that close the loop between data and action. Those principles apply regardless of the tools available to you.
